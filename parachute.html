Modern Parachute

Defined Deceleration // 1783

Inventor: Louis-Sébastien Lenormand Year: 1783 Concept: Controlled Descent

In 1783, Louis-Sébastien Lenormand jumped from the tower of the Montpellier observatory using a rigid-framed parachute of his own design. While basic concepts had existed earlier, Lenormand is credited with the first modern demonstration and for coining the word "parachute" (from the Greek para "against" and French chute "fall").

"The goal is not to fall, but to arrive with the grace of a leaf."

Engineering Safety

Early parachutes were rigid structures made of silk and wood. Lenormand's breakthrough was the realization that air resistance could be harnessed consistently to limit the speed of a falling body to survivable levels.

Evolution

Within years, André-Jacques Garnerin developed the first non-rigid (frameless) silk parachute, allowing for foldable designs used today.

Legacy

The parachute became essential for the development of both military and civilian aviation, saving thousands of lives.
