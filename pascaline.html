Pascaline

The Dawn of Computing // 1642

Inventor: Blaise Pascal Year: 1642 Origin: Rouen, France

In 1642, at the young age of 19, Blaise Pascal invented the Pascaline, the first mechanical calculator to be used in an office. Motivated by the arduous task of his father's tax calculations, Pascal designed a machine that could add and subtract directly and multiply and divide by repetition.

"The arithmetical machine produces effects which approach nearer to thought than all the actions of animals."

Engineering Brilliance

The Pascaline was a masterpiece of 17th-century engineering. It consisted of a series of wheels, each with ten teeth representing the digits 0 to 9. A sophisticated "sautoir" mechanism handled the carrying of digits from one wheel to the next, a problem that had plagued earlier attempts at calculating machines.

Succession

It laid the mathematical foundation for later machines by Leibniz and, much later, the analytical engines of the 19th century.

Precision

The machine was capable of handling numbers up to hundreds of thousands, a feat of incredible mechanical precision for its era.

Legacy

While the Pascaline was not a commercial success due to its high cost and complexity, it remain a landmark in the history of technology. It demonstrated that mathematical operations could be outsourced to machines, marking the true birth of the information age.
